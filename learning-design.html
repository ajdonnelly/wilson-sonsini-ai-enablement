The instructional-design lens

Turning a capability into adoption

Great enablement isn’t a feature tour — it’s behavior change. When I design training for a new AI tool, every choice is a learning-design choice: start from the practitioner’s real work, have them practice the skill rather than watch it, and make the answer available at the moment of need. That is the difference between a session people click through and training that changes how they practice.

Why this matters for the role

An AI Enablement Specialist has to know the tools and make other people fluent and confident with them — at scale, past a single session, into a durable firmwide habit. That second half is a craft. My graduate training is in exactly that: how people learn complex, high-stakes information.

The principles under the hood

The training looks simple on purpose. Underneath, each interaction is doing deliberate instructional work.

Scaffolding From weak to strong, in steps

A build-a-prompt activity moves the learner from a vague prompt to a structured one piece at a time, so complexity rises only as fast as confidence does.

Retrieval Practice beats exposure

Short knowledge checks after each concept force the learner to recall and apply, not just recognize. Retrieval is what moves a skill from “I saw that” to “I can do that.”

Authentic assessment Real work, real stakes

A spot-the-issue activity puts a plausible AI-drafted passage next to its source and asks the learner to catch the failure — the exact judgment they’ll need on the job, rehearsed safely.

Transfer Built to leave the room

Training ends with a prompt-library template the learner keeps. Learning that stays in the session doesn’t count; the goal is a habit and an asset that outlive it.

About

I sit where the science, the law, and the learning meet

Answering that question requires all three parts of my background: the technical pipeline, from anonymized imaging data through segmentation, modeling, and fabrication; the instructional design question of what this audience actually needs to understand and how we would know whether they understood it; and the legal context of what a factfinder is being asked to decide and on what evidence.

That is the through line of my work: taking something technically correct and making it genuinely understandable to the person who has to act on it. AI enablement is the same problem in a different form.

I already do that work inside a law firm. As a senior litigation paralegal, I became one of our first enterprise Copilot power users and now serve on the firm’s AI committee. Working with a change champion in another office, I helped design our enterprise AI pilot around the people doing the work and the workflows they actually use. I helped develop the proposal, present it to the committee, test tools such as Copilot, Claude, and Filevine’s LOIS in real legal workflows, and lead sessions and office hours for colleagues ranging from staff members to partners.

I have also spent years learning what it takes to make adoption stick. For more than a decade, I helped run a boutique law firm from the inside and led major system migrations, including selecting, configuring, and rolling out new platforms and then helping busy legal professionals incorporate them into their daily work. Evaluating technology, piloting it, and driving adoption are not abstract concepts to me.

That requires the same discipline AI demands: careful source review, verification, judgment, and an understanding that speed only matters when the answer remains reliable.

Technical fluency

I can hold the technical conversation, not just translate it

An enablement specialist who cannot evaluate the technology ends up relaying vendor claims. I work from how these systems actually behave — what retrieval does and does not guarantee, where a model fails, and how you would prove it on your own matters before anyone stakes work on it.

01 Retrieval, grounding, and why “it cited something” is not enough

Most legal AI is retrieval-augmented: the model answers from documents fetched at query time, so the quality of the answer is bounded by the quality of the retrieval. That means the failure mode worth training people on is not only the invented citation — it is the confident answer built on the wrong or incomplete set of retrieved documents. I teach practitioners to check what the tool actually looked at, not just whether the output looks authoritative.

02 Evaluating a tool against real work, not a demo

A vendor demo is a curated best case. A useful evaluation runs the tool on representative matters you already know the answers to, and scores it: was it accurate, was it checkable, did it save real time, did it hold up on the messy inputs rather than the clean ones? A gold-set of known-answer matters turns “the attorneys liked it” into evidence — and gives you a baseline to re-test against when the model changes underneath you.

03 Prompt and workflow design as an engineering practice

Prompting is not incantation. It is specification: give the model the role, the source material, the constraints, the output shape, and a place to say “I don’t know.” The durable artifact is not a clever prompt — it is a documented workflow with a human-review checkpoint at the step where being wrong is expensive. I build those, run them on real work, and hand them over so someone else can run them too.

Background & training

Four things that rarely sit in one person

Instructional design, hands-on technical work with scientific data, a decade inside law firms, and a J.D. focused on AI law and governance. Enablement roles usually get one or two of these. The reason they belong together is that the problem is always the same: a technically correct output that a human has to understand, trust, and be accountable for.

01 Instructional design — making hard things learnable

Three graduate programs circling one question: how people take in, make sense of, and act on complex information — and how it gets misunderstood. That is the first problem AI enablement poses, on day one, with every audience. It is why the training in this portfolio is designed rather than assembled: scaffolded, with retrieval practice, authentic tasks, and a verification habit built in.

02 Technical work with scientific data · M.S. Biomedical Visualization (BVIS)

BVIS is one of only five accredited programs of its kind in North America. My work there is a build pipeline, not a drawing class: anonymized DICOM imaging data → segmentation in Mimics → digital model refinement → print preparation and fabrication → a physical object someone can hold and reason about. Every step is an editorial and technical decision about fidelity, simplification, and what a specific audience actually needs. It is the same discipline an AI workflow demands — and the same data-handling rigor, since the whole pipeline runs on de-identified data.

03 The thesis — where the science, the law, and the learning meet

My graduate research (H3Dp) asks whether haptic, self-guided 3D-printed models improve how jurors comprehend expert medical testimony — a comprehension gap that can decide cases. It is instructional design, technical fabrication, and the legal system in one problem. It also produced the conviction behind how I think about legal AI: point-of-care manufactured devices raise exactly the questions legal AI is now asking — who validates the output, what counts as sufficient testing, and where accountability sits when the tool is novel. Medicine has spent decades building those frameworks. I expect legal AI governance to rhyme with medical-device regulation more than most people currently assume, and both resolve to the same principle: new technology earns trust through accountable human review.

04 Law firm experience, and a J.D. focused on AI law & governance

More than a decade inside firms — running one end to end, and now a senior litigation paralegal on a firm’s AI committee — means I already speak the language of matters, deadlines, privilege, and why an attorney says no. The Executive J.D. (AI Law & Governance, University of Hawaiʻi, matriculating Fall 2026) adds the layer that decides what a firm can safely deploy. Enablement that ignores governance is how firms get into trouble; I intend to be fluent in both halves.
